Home Office — Regulatory References

IRC §280A(c)(1) — Exclusive Use Test: Space must be used regularly and exclusively for business. No personal use permitted at any time. A dedicated room is ideal; partitioned area within a room may qualify with careful documentation.
Rev. Proc. 2013-13 — Simplified Method: $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft ($1,500 max deduction). No depreciation recapture on sale. No carryforward. Cannot exceed gross income from business.
Form 8829 — Regular Method: Actual home expenses × business-use percentage. Depreciation is mandatory if deduction claimed. Unused deduction carries forward indefinitely under IRC §280A(c)(5).
§1250 Depreciation Recapture: When home is sold, all depreciation claimed under the regular method is recaptured at up to 25% rate (unrecaptured §1250 gain), even if home qualifies for §121 exclusion. Simplified method avoids this issue.
Principal Place of Business Test: For nurses with multiple work sites, home office qualifies if used for administrative/management activities AND no other fixed location exists for those activities — Soliman v. Commissioner, 506 U.S. 168 (1993); IRC §280A(c)(1)(A).

Governing IRC Code Sections

IRC §1361 — S-Corp Eligibility
Defines eligible S-Corps: domestic corp, ≤100 shareholders, one class of stock, shareholders must be US citizens/residents or certain trusts. LLCs can elect S-Corp tax treatment while remaining an LLC under state law.
IRC §1362(a) — S Election
Election made on Form 2553, signed by all shareholders. Must be filed by the 15th day of the 3rd month of the tax year to be effective for that year. Late relief under Rev. Proc. 2013-30.
IRC §3121(b)(3) — FICA Exemption
S-Corp distributions to shareholders are not "wages" and therefore not subject to FICA (Social Security + Medicare taxes). This is the core tax arbitrage mechanism. Salary/compensation is still subject to FICA.
IRC §1402(a) — SE Tax Base
Self-employment income includes net earnings from self-employment. S-Corp distributions are explicitly excluded from SE income — only the W-2 wages are subject to employment taxes.
IRC §199A — QBI Deduction
20% deduction on qualified business income. Available to both LLC and S-Corp (made permanent by OBBBA 2025). S-Corp wages count toward W-2 wage limitation test at higher income levels (>$394,600 MFJ).

Key Cases & IRS Guidance

Rev. Rul. 74-44
IRS ruling establishing that a sole shareholder-employee of an S-Corp must receive reasonable compensation before taking distributions. Forms the legal basis of the reasonable compensation requirement.
Radtke v. United States, 895 F.2d 1196 (7th Cir. 1990)
IRS successfully recharacterized $0 salary to physician-shareholder as wages. Court upheld. Established that S-Corp distributions cannot substitute for reasonable compensation in service businesses.
Watson v. United States, 668 F.3d 1008 (8th Cir. 2012)
IRS recharacterized $24,000 salary (on $200k+ profit) as wages. CPA's S-Corp compensation was unreasonably low. Court found $93,000 was reasonable — reinforcing the 25-35% guideline for professional services.
IRS S-Corp Audit Considerations
IRS actively audits S-Corps with low/no officer compensation (see IRS MSSP — S-Corp Audit Technique Guide). Best defense: documented board resolution, BLS wage comparable, consistent payroll history.
Rev. Proc. 2013-30 — Late Election Relief
Provides simplified procedure for late S election if failure was inadvertent. Can often obtain relief retroactively. Requires that all shareholders consent and entity operated as if S-Corp from formation date.

Trust Types & Use Cases

Revocable Living Trust

Avoids probate, maintains privacy, and allows seamless asset transfer at death. Grantor retains full control during lifetime. Does NOT reduce estate taxes — assets remain in taxable estate. Ideal for simplifying administration, especially for complex asset portfolios or real estate in multiple states.

Irrevocable Trust

Assets transferred out of grantor's estate — can reduce estate tax. Common types: Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT), Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT), Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT). Trade-off: grantor loses control. Best for high-net-worth estates approaching the exemption threshold.

GRAT — Grantor Retained Annuity Trust

Grantor transfers assets into trust, receives annuity payments for a term. Appreciation above the IRS hurdle rate (§7520 rate) passes to beneficiaries estate-tax-free. Zero-out GRAT technique can transfer significant wealth. Risk: grantor must survive the trust term.

Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

Grantor transfers appreciated assets to trust, receives income stream for life or term, and charity receives remainder. Benefits: immediate partial charitable deduction, avoids immediate capital gains on appreciated property, diversification opportunity. Popular for highly appreciated business interests or real estate.

Tax Planning for High-Income Individuals (>$150k)

At higher income levels, each dollar saved in tax has compounding value. The strategies below are generally available regardless of entity type and should be evaluated annually.

1. Maximize Pre-Tax Retirement Contributions

For self-employed individuals, a Solo 401(k) is the most powerful vehicle — allowing up to $24,500 employee deferral plus employer contributions of 25% of net compensation, capped at $70,000 total (2026). If over age 50, add $8,000 catch-up ($11,250 if ages 60–63 under SECURE 2.0). At a 32% marginal rate, a $70,000 Solo 401(k) contribution saves approximately $22,400 in federal tax that year. Account must be established by December 31.

Consider a Defined Benefit Plan if income exceeds $300k and you are age 50+ — contributions can exceed $150,000/year depending on age and actuarial assumptions. Often combined with a Solo 401(k) for maximum sheltering.

Authority: IRC §401(a), §402(g), §415; IRS Pub. 560

2. S-Corporation Election to Reduce SE Tax

Self-employment tax (15.3% on the first $176,100, 2.9% above) is the largest single tax burden for high-earning sole proprietors. By electing S-Corp status, only the reasonable compensation portion is subject to FICA — distributions are not. At $285k net income with 28% reasonable comp (~$80k salary), the SE tax reduction is approximately $18,000/year.

Use the S-Corp Analyzer (sidebar: Entity & S-Corp Strategy) to model your specific savings. File Form 2553 by March 15 of the tax year. Reasonable compensation should be documented annually; IRS guidance suggests 25–35% of net pass-through income for professional service businesses.

Authority: IRC §§1361, 1362, 3121(b)(3); Rev. Rul. 74-44; Watson v. US (8th Cir. 2012)

3. Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) Planning — §1411

The 3.8% NIIT applies to the lesser of net investment income or the amount AGI exceeds $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ). Investment income includes dividends, interest, capital gains, rents, and passive activity income.

Mitigation strategies: (1) Move investments into tax-exempt municipal bonds; (2) Maximize retirement plan contributions to reduce MAGI below threshold; (3) Invest in Qualified Opportunity Zones (OZ) — deferred and potentially excluded gains; (4) Real estate professional status (750 hours, more than 50% of services) converts rental income from passive to non-passive, removing it from NIIT base; (5) Qualified Opportunity Zone Funds for capital gain deferral.

Authority: IRC §1411; Reg. §1.1411-1 through -10; IRS Publication 550

4. Backdoor Roth IRA Strategy

When MAGI exceeds $150,000 (single) / $236,500 (MFJ) in 2026, direct Roth IRA contributions phase out. The backdoor strategy: (1) Contribute $7,000 to a nondeductible Traditional IRA; (2) Convert immediately to Roth. If you have no other pre-tax IRA balances, the pro-rata rule does not apply and the conversion is essentially tax-free.

Pro-Rata Rule Warning: If you have other pre-tax IRA funds (rollover, deductible contributions), the conversion is taxed proportionally. Consider rolling pre-tax IRA funds into your Solo 401(k) to isolate the nondeductible basis before converting.

Authority: IRC §408(d)(1); Notice 2014-54; IRS Form 8606

5. Qualified Business Income (QBI) §199A Optimization

Under OBBBA 2025, §199A is now permanent — a major win for self-employed professionals. For non-SSTB businesses below the income threshold ($197,300 single / $394,600 MFJ in 2026), the deduction is simply 20% of QBI. For SSTB businesses (health, law, consulting, financial services) near the threshold, strategies include:

  • Maximize retirement contributions to reduce taxable income below the SSTB threshold
  • Consider a spouse's W-2 wages — adds to W-2 wage limitation at higher incomes
  • Split activities: if you have a non-SSTB component (e.g., rental of office space to your own practice), segment it
  • For S-Corps above the threshold: pay reasonable W-2 wages (50% of W-2 wages or 25% + 2.5% of unadjusted basis must exceed 20% of QBI)

Authority: IRC §199A; Reg. §1.199A-1 through -6; OBBBA 2025 §70001

6. Augusta Rule — IRC §280A(g)

IRC §280A(g) allows a homeowner to rent their home for up to 14 days per year without recognizing the rental income on their personal return. When your business pays you rent for bona fide meetings held at your home, the business deducts the expense and you personally receive the income tax-free.

Implementation: Research local comparable commercial meeting space rates (hotels, conference centers). Have your S-Corp or business pay fair-market rent for documented meetings. Issue a 1099-MISC if over $600. Keep minutes of each meeting at the home. At $500/day × 14 days = $7,000 tax-free personal income, $7,000 business deduction. Net benefit at 32% rate ≈ $2,240/year.

Key limitation: Must be 14 days or fewer. The business must have a legitimate business purpose for each meeting. S-Corp or C-Corp structure required (not sole proprietor — related party rules apply).

Authority: IRC §280A(g); Rev. Proc. 2004-34; See also Fankhanel v. Commissioner

Tax Planning for Small Business Owners — Schedule C / S-Corp

1. 100% Bonus Depreciation on Equipment & Qualified Property

Under OBBBA 2025, 100% bonus depreciation is permanent. Any qualified business property placed in service in the tax year — computers, medical equipment, furniture, vehicles over 6,000 lbs — can be fully expensed in year one. Combined with §179, this is the most powerful single-year deduction available to small businesses.

Example: A physician buys $80,000 of medical equipment in December. Under bonus depreciation, the entire $80,000 is deductible in year one, saving approximately $25,600 at a 32% rate — vs. $16,000 over 5 years under regular depreciation.

Vehicles: SUVs over 6,000 lbs GVWR qualify for full §179 expensing up to $28,900 (2026) plus bonus depreciation on remainder. Luxury auto limits cap smaller vehicles at ~$12,800/year.

Authority: IRC §168(k); §179; OBBBA 2025 §70002; Rev. Proc. 2024-13

2. Health Insurance Deduction — Self-Employed

Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health, dental, and long-term care insurance premiums as an above-the-line adjustment to income (Form 1040, Schedule 1, Line 17). This deduction reduces AGI — meaning it also reduces SE tax base and NIIT exposure. Premiums for the taxpayer, spouse, and dependents all qualify.

Limitation: Cannot exceed net profit from the business. Cannot be taken if eligible for employer-subsidized coverage through a spouse's employer.

An S-Corp shareholder-employee must have the corporation pay or reimburse premiums and include them in W-2 wages as compensation — then deduct on Form 1040. This two-step is required to get both the FICA exclusion and the personal deduction.

Authority: IRC §162(l); Rev. Rul. 91-26; IRS Notice 2008-1

3. HSA — Health Savings Account Strategy

An HSA paired with a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) provides a triple tax benefit: contributions are deductible, growth is tax-free, withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. No "use it or lose it" — balances roll over indefinitely.

2026 limits: $4,300 self-only / $8,550 family (+$1,000 catch-up if 55+). At a 32% marginal rate, a $4,300 HSA contribution saves $1,376 in federal tax — plus avoids SE tax on the deduction if structured correctly through an S-Corp.

After age 65, HSA funds can be withdrawn for any purpose without penalty (only ordinary income tax applies) — making it effectively a second IRA. Invest HSA balances aggressively for long-term growth.

Authority: IRC §223; IRS Publication 969; Rev. Proc. 2025-19

4. Business Meal & Entertainment Deductions — §274

Post-2017 TCJA rules: Entertainment expenses are 0% deductible (tickets to events, golf, sporting events). Business meals remain 50% deductible if (1) not lavish, (2) business is discussed, (3) you are present, and (4) you document the business purpose and attendees.

100% deductible meals: meals provided on employer premises for the convenience of the employer (office snacks, team meals where overnight travel is required), company holiday parties open to all employees, meals that are treated as compensation to employees (included in W-2).

Key documentation: Business purpose, attendees/relationship, date, place. Keep receipts for all meals over $75. Use a business credit card to create an audit trail. A contemporaneous log (same-day or within the week) is more defensible than reconstructed records.

Authority: IRC §274; Reg. §1.274-12; TCJA §13304; IRS Notice 2018-76

5. Qualified Opportunity Zone (QOZ) Investments

Reinvesting capital gains into a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) within 180 days defers the gain until December 31, 2026 (or earlier disposition). Gains held in QOFs for 10+ years are permanently excluded from tax. This is one of the most powerful capital gain elimination tools available.

Example: Sell a practice asset with a $200,000 capital gain. Invest in a QOF within 180 days. The $200k gain is deferred (recognized Dec 31, 2026). If the QOF investment grows to $400k and is held 10+ years, the $200k appreciation is permanently tax-free.

Caution: QOZ investments carry real estate and operational risk. Only invest capital that can be committed long-term. Due diligence on the fund sponsor is essential.

Authority: IRC §1400Z-1, §1400Z-2; Reg. §1.1400Z2(a)-1 through -4; IRS Form 8997

Solo 401(k) vs SEP-IRA — Which is Better for You?

The Solo 401(k) is almost always superior for self-employed individuals with no full-time employees because of the employee deferral component. A SEP-IRA is limited to 25% of net compensation from the first dollar, while a Solo 401(k) allows you to contribute the first $24,500 of income as employee deferral before the 25% employer component applies — dramatically higher total contributions at lower income levels.

Example at $80,000 net income: SEP-IRA allows ~$14,800 (25% × ~$59k net comp after ½ SE tax). Solo 401(k) allows up to $24,500 employee + $14,800 employer = $39,300. The Solo 401(k) shelters $24,500 more per year — saving $7,840 in federal tax at 32%.

The only advantage of a SEP-IRA is administrative simplicity — it's a single account with no annual filing requirement (Form 5500-EZ not required). For anyone serious about tax reduction, the Solo 401(k) is the clear winner. Deadlines: Establish account by December 31; fund by tax return due date including extensions.

Strategies for Ages 50–65 — Catch-Up Contributions & Social Security Timing

For clients ages 50+, catch-up contributions substantially increase the annual tax shelter. SECURE 2.0 creates a "super catch-up" for ages 60–63: $11,250 additional to 401(k) instead of the standard $8,000. This window is valuable — max contributions in these years when income is typically highest.

Social Security timing: Delaying SS benefits from age 62 to 70 increases the monthly benefit by approximately 8% per year in delayed credits. For high-income self-employed clients still working, deferring makes sense. Model the breakeven age — typically around 80–82. Also consider that SS benefits received while still working may be partially taxable.

Roth Conversion ladder: If retiring in your 60s with significant pre-tax IRA/401(k) balances, consider systematic Roth conversions in the years between retirement and when Social Security / RMDs begin — filling up the 12% or 22% bracket at low effective rates. Each dollar converted is a future RMD eliminated.

Home Office Deduction — Complete Rules (IRC §280A)

Simplified Method (Rev. Proc. 2013-13)

  • · $5 per sq ft of dedicated home office space
  • · Maximum 300 sq ft → max deduction $1,500/year
  • · No depreciation recapture on sale of home
  • · No carryforward of unused deductions
  • · Choose simplified vs. actual each year

Regular (Actual Expense) Method — Form 8829

  • · Deduct business % of: mortgage interest, rent, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation
  • · Business % = office sq ft ÷ total home sq ft
  • · Can exceed $1,500 — often $3,000–$8,000+
  • · Depreciation component creates recapture at sale (§1250, 25% unrecaptured rate)
  • · Unused deductions carry forward to future years

IRS Examples — Qualifying Home Office Use Cases

Plumber (IRS Pub 587, Example 1): Plumber has no fixed business location. Uses a room at home exclusively and regularly for administrative work — scheduling, billing, record keeping. Does plumbing work at client sites. The home office qualifies as principal place of business because it is the only fixed location for admin functions.
Sales Representative (Example 2): Sales rep has no office provided by employer. Uses a room at home exclusively for business. Meets clients at their locations. Home office qualifies — primary business location for admin. No requirement that clients visit the home.
Anesthesiologist (Soliman v. Commissioner, 1993): Anesthesiologist performed services at multiple hospitals. Used home office for admin, billing, and study. Supreme Court held the home office was NOT the principal place of business because the most important activities (administering anesthesia) occurred at hospitals. Post-Soliman fix: If administrative activities are substantial and no other fixed location is available for those functions, the home office qualifies even if the "core" work is done elsewhere. Nursing consultants who perform client visits but manage their business from home generally qualify.
Attorney (Example 3): Attorney sees clients in a rented office 3 days/week and uses a home office the other 2 days. The home office does NOT qualify as principal place of business because there is another fixed location (the rented office) where substantial business activities occur. Home office must be the only fixed location or used for clearly distinct administrative functions.
Itemized vs. Standard Deduction — When to Itemize

For 2026, standard deductions are: $15,000 (single) / $30,000 (MFJ) / $22,500 (HOH). Itemize only if your allowable deductions exceed this. Common itemized deductions: mortgage interest (Form 1098, limited to $750k loan balance), state/local taxes (SALT — capped at $40,000 under OBBBA), charitable contributions (cash: up to 60% of AGI; appreciated stock: up to 30% of AGI), medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI.

Bunching strategy: If itemized deductions are consistently just below the standard deduction, consider "bunching" — accelerating 2 years of charitable giving into one year (or using a Donor Advised Fund), then taking the standard deduction the next year. This creates above-average deduction years without a permanent increase in giving.

Charitable Deduction Strategies — Donor Advised Funds & Appreciated Stock

Two highly tax-efficient charitable strategies: (1) Donor Advised Fund (DAF): Contribute a lump sum to a DAF (Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, Vanguard Charitable) in a high-income year. Take the full deduction now. Grant funds to charities over time. Use appreciated stock to fund the DAF — avoid capital gains and get full fair-market-value deduction.

(2) Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD): If age 70½+, transfer up to $105,000/year directly from IRA to charity. The distribution counts toward your RMD but is excluded from gross income — effectively a deduction even if you take the standard deduction. This is the best way to give charitably once you have required minimum distributions.

SALT $40,000 Cap — Who Benefits?

The SALT cap increase from $10,000 to $40,000 is significant for high-income taxpayers in high-tax states (CA, NY, NJ, MA, IL). A California resident with $145k income might pay $12,000+ in state income tax — previously capped at $10k, now fully deductible.

Phase-out: The $40,000 cap phases out for AGI above $500,000 — reducing by $1 for each $10 of excess AGI. At $540,000 AGI, the SALT cap is back to $36,000; at $900,000+ it phases back to $10,000.

For NY clients (Scenario 3 — Linda Park), combined NY state + NYC tax can approach $65,000+. Even with the $40,000 cap, she recovers a meaningful deduction that was capped at $10,000 under TCJA.

Estate Tax Exemption at $15M — Planning Implications

The federal estate/gift tax exemption is now $15M per person ($30M per married couple), permanent under OBBBA. For most self-employed professionals under age 60 with net worth below $10M, federal estate tax is no longer an immediate planning concern.

The planning focus shifts: Income tax basis planning becomes more important than estate tax minimization. Strategies that "freeze" an estate (GRATs, irrevocable trusts) must now be weighed against the loss of stepped-up basis at death. A revocable trust is still valuable for probate avoidance and privacy.

State estate taxes: Many states (MA, OR, WA, IL, MD) have their own estate tax with exemptions as low as $1M–$2M. High-net-worth clients in these states still face state estate tax planning needs regardless of the federal exemption increase.

QBI Deduction Now Permanent — 5-Year Planning Horizon

With §199A permanent, the 20% QBI deduction should now be factored into all multi-year income projections. The effective top rate on qualified business income for a pass-through entity is 29.6% (37% × 80% QBI factor), significantly below the 37% corporate dividend rate chain.

For S-Corp shareholders, this means: distributions remain more tax-efficient than additional salary at high income levels — as long as reasonable compensation is properly documented. The S-Corp + QBI combination is now a permanent tax arbitrage strategy, not a temporary planning opportunity.

SSTB clients (health, law, financial services) should model income carefully to stay below the phase-out threshold or ensure deduction qualification through W-2 wages / property tests. If income is expected to grow above the threshold, consider expanding into non-SSTB activities or hiring employees to meet the W-2 wage test.
